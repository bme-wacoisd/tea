## Slide 4

### Vocabulary

- 4

| Assessment | Any tool used to collect, analyze, and respond to information about student understanding of learning targets |
| --- | --- |
| Formative Assessment | Low or no-stakes assessments that gather data about a small number (1-2) learning target. |
| Summative Assessment | High-stakes or graded assessments, given at the end of a learning sequence, that gather data about student mastery of multiple learning targets. |
| Low-stakes | An assessment that represents a small percentage of a student’s grade |
| No-stakes | An assessment that does not count towards a student’s grade |
| High-stakes | An assessment that represents a large percentage of a student’s grade |

## Slide 5

### Assessments

- A tool used to
- collect data about student understanding
- help a teacher make instructional decisions
- communicate progress with students
- Examples of Assessments:
- Tests, quizzes, projects, essays, exit tickets, journals, class discussions, polls, self-assessments, graphic organizers
- 5

---

## Slide 6

### Formative Assessments

- Low or no-stakes
- Allow the teacher to collect data throughout the learning process
- Usually measures 1–2 objectives
- Help students move information from short-term to long-term memory
- Classroom discussions, journals, graphic organizers, anticipation guides, exit tickets, polls, quick writes, summaries, low- or no-stakes quizzes
- 6

---

## Slide 7

### Summative Assessments

- High stakes (graded)
- Used at the end of a learning sequence
- Evaluate student achievement of learning targets
- Usually measures multiple learning targets
- Tests, high-stakes quizzes, projects, essays
- 7

## Slide 8

### Exit Ticket

- 1. Define formative assessment and summative assessment and explain the purpose of each.
- 2. Match the descriptions below to the appropriate assessment type.
- Assessment #1
- Students are working independently on a set of math problems from the day’s lesson. Students are expected to subtract using the take from ten strategy. While they work, the teacher circulates the room to observe and take notes. She uses a checklist to record common areas of understanding and common misconceptions.
- Assessment #2
- Fifth grade math students are engaged in a unit about financial literacy, in which they learn about income, taxes, and budgeting. Throughout the unit, students have solved real-world problems with fractions, decimals, and percentages. The teacher assigns each student a graded project to create a budget for a household. Students are expected to accurately calculate income, taxes, and expenses. Students also need to describe how to balance a budget when income exceeds expenses.
