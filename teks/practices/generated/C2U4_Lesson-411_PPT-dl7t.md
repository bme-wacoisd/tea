## Slide 6

### Stretch It

- Reward a “right” answer with a more challenging gauge student thinking and help students build long-term memory
- Push students to answer with precision and confidence
- Set the tone for a classroom where no answer is taken for granted and students are always searching for why.
- Establish a classroom culture that values a growth mindset

## Slide 7

### Types of Directive Prompts

- 7

| Prompt | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Ask How or Why | Teacher: What is the perimeter of the rectangle? Student: Fourteen centimeters. Teacher: How did you get that? |
| Ask for Another Way to Reach the Same Answer | Teacher: How did you get your answer? Student: I got 95 by counting up 4 from 86, then adding the remaining 5 in 9 to get 95. Teacher: Nice! Can you think of another way to get the same answer? |
| Ask for More Precise Expression | Teacher: How did Terri respond when she saw the photograph? Student: Terri was surprised when she saw the photograph. Teacher: What's an even stronger word for "surprised?" |
| Ask for Evidence | Teacher: What’s one way to describe Hamlet? Student: He is indecisive. He’s hesitant to act. Teacher: What lines in this scene show he is indecisive? |
| Ask for Integration of a Related Skill | Teacher: What’s 7+5? Student: 12. Teacher: What number sentence could you use to confirm that’s correct? |
| Ask for Application of the Same Skill in a New Setting | Teacher: What is the perimeter of the square? Student: Twenty centimeters. Teacher: If this were a pentagon instead of a square, what would the perimeter be? |
