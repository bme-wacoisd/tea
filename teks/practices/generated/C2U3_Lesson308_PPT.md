## Slide 4

### Introduction to School Community Support Roles

- Counselors: Support students emotional needs, can provide support for social situations, may know career goals
- Resource Teachers: Teachers who provide strategies for teaching different populations or specific content areas, such as: ELL Teachers, Special Education Teachers, Reading/Literacy Specialists, Math Specialists.
- Social Worker: Support students outside of school with family and community, can provide mental health services.

## Slide 5

### Introduction to School Community Support Roles

- Previous Teachers: Can inform on student’s strengths and opportunities for growth, successful strategies, seating arrangements, hobbies, interests, performance on tests and assignments, grouping preferences. Current teachers have a responsibility to use an asset-based mindset when learning about a student. If the previous teacher did not have a positive relationship with the student, the current teacher should consider that when collecting information
- Enrichment Teachers: In many schools, the art, music, pe teachers, and librarians have known and taught the students for years. They are an excellent resource for knowledge of students.
- After School Teacher/Coaches: Another good resource for classroom teachers to learn about student personalities, talent, and interests. Collaborating with them can help teachers find more ways to motivate students.

## Slide 6

### How to Build Partnerships with School & Community Support Professionals

- Build relationships with colleagues
- Understand the colleague’s role
- Ask questions about ways that their work can be supported by your role as the teacher
- Respect their time
- Arrange a time to meet
- Preplan questions
- Send questions in advance if possible
- Unless you have a good relationship with that colleague, do not assume they have time to meet.
- Respect their expertise
- Ask questions and listen
- Be mindful to maintain an asset-based mindset about your students
- another teacher may not have a positive relationship with a student, but you should never pre-judge a student based on that experience

- Who are some of the colleagues that can help you to support your students instructionally?
