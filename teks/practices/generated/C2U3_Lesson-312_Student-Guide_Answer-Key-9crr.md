What are some things about you that your teachers are likely not aware of, but might be helpful for them to know as you interact throughout the school year?
Answers will vary, but will likely include students’ passions and talents, family structures and home life, cultures and traditions, past academic performance, hobbies and extracurriculars, and volunteer work or employment history.

What are some ways in which teachers may be able to learn more about their students?
personal conversations, interest inventories and student profiles, classroom icebreaker activities, formative assessments, conversations with other staff members, home visits and family interviews, academic records, artifacts from outside stakeholders.

What types of instructional decisions might teachers make in order for instruction to become more effective when cognizant of students’ funds of knowledge?
Answers will vary but will likely include:  creating culturally relevant experiences for students, promoting trust and safe learning environments, delve into students’ curiosities, and enable students to take ownership over their learning.

Using Qualitative and Quantitative Data to Make Instructional Decisions
Qualitative and quantitative data can be used to make instructional decisions about:
Designing assignments given to let students show what they have learned.
Engaging students in their learning
Identifying when students need help.
Supporting them effectively when needed

| Ms. H has assigned an individual project for the week. At the end of class on Friday, all students need to turn in their assignment. How should Ms. H check-in with Grover? | Ms. H checks in privately with Grover during class on Tuesday to see if he needs help. Ms. H will check in private with Grover towards the end of the week to ensure quality of work and that he is double-checking responses. | Grover doesn’t like saying that he needs help in front of his peers.  Also, Ms. H knows that he’ll probably work on the harder questions first and may try to work quickly. |
| Ms. H notices that Grover has already finished his math assignment while other students in the class are struggling. What should she do? | Ms. H might have Grover work with a small group or with individual students that are struggling | Ms. H knows that Grover often learns best when he has the opportunity to share his learning and assist his peers. |
| Grover is working on an assignment with three of his classmates. His three group members want to stay after-school to ask Ms. H for help with their assignment. What should Ms. H do? | Ms. H asks the group members if they can meet with her during lunch or recess to work on their assignment. | Ms. H knows that Grover won’t be able to stay after-school because he must walk his sister home. To avoid him being left out of the group, she tries to find another time to provide extra help. |
| Grover has been working hard to improve in social studies, but he’s been struggling.  He’s not feeling confident in his academic skills. | As students are working in centers during math, Ms. H asks Grover to help students at the most challenging center. | Ms. H wants to help Grover be more confident. He enjoys helping his peers with math, and it allows him to demonstrate his skill in this topic. |
| Ms. H needs to help students review for an upcoming vocabulary quiz. What strategy might she employ? | Ms. H might want to make the review a competition or a game. | Ms. H knows that Grover enjoys competing and that he will likely be very engaged. |
| Grover has expressed concern over a social studies project on citizenship. He seems unmotivated and disengaged. What should Ms. H do? | Ms. H might encourage Grover to infuse the project with his creativity and passion for art. Ms. H might ask Grover to learn about athletes that have become American citizens. Ms. H could have students present their work to show what they have learned. | Ms. H knows that Grover is very artistic and will likely be excited to bring creativity to the project. Ms. H knows that Grover likes sports and may find it interesting to learn more about some of his favorite athletes. Ms. H knows that Grover likes to share his learning with peers. |
