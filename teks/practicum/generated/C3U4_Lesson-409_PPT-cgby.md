## Slide 4

### Vocabulary


| Universal Design for Learning (UDL) | An educational framework to guide the development of flexible learning environments that can accommodate individual learning differences through engagement, representation, and expression. UDL minimizes barriers and maximizes learning for all students. |

## Slide 5

### Multiple Means of Engagement

- Allowing students choice in how they might reach a learning target
- Providing opportunities for students to engage in learning experiences that are relevant and valuable to their goals and individual interests
- Creating a supportive environment where students feel safe to learn and take risks
- 5

---

## Slide 6

### Multiple Means of Representation

- Offering options for customizing the display of information
- Offering alternatives for auditory information
- Offering alternatives for visual information
- 6

---

## Slide 7

### Multiple Means of Action and Expression

- Allowing students to share their learning in different ways
- Providing alternative ways for students to interact with instructional materials and their environment
- Providing access to and support with tools and assistive technologies
- Providing opportunities for students to communicate using multiple forms of media
- Supporting learners in setting challenging and authentic goals
- Provide flexible tools and processes to support organization and memory

### Exit Ticket

- Consider your own field experience during your classroom visits. What examples of UDL strategies have you seen your teacher (or other teachers) use?
- Mr. L is teaching his fourth-grade math students how to add decimal numbers. The students are doing well with the content. Therefore, as an in-class activity to show their mastery, Mr. L asks students to write their own word problem using decimals and demonstrate how to solve the problem. How does this assignment meet the criteria for UDL guidelines? How does it not? What else could Mr. L do to make this assignment more accessible for all students?
