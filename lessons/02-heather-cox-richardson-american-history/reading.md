# Reading: Heather Cox Richardson

*Follow along as your instructor reads this passage aloud.*

---

**The most important idea in this reading:** History doesn't repeat itself—it rhymes. Understanding the past helps students make sense of the present. That's not a bonus. That's the point.

Every night, a history professor in Boston sits down and writes. By morning, over two million people have read what she wrote. Heather Cox Richardson doesn't write textbooks. She writes letters—"Letters from an American"—connecting today's headlines to patterns from the past. A Supreme Court decision echoes an 1876 ruling. A political speech borrows rhetoric from the 1930s. Her readers don't just learn history. They start seeing it everywhere.

Richardson learned to think this way at Phillips Exeter Academy, where students sit around a table and discuss—no lectures, no memorizing dates. Everyone participates. Everyone's voice matters. She took that approach into her career as a professor at Boston College, and eventually into her newsletter. Her philosophy is simple: "If people have true facts in front of them, they will make good decisions. They will not necessarily be the decisions that I would make, but they're the decisions that make sense for them." She trusts her readers. She trusts her students. That trust is the foundation.

What makes Richardson effective isn't what she knows—it's how she shares it. She writes clearly. She avoids jargon. She sounds like a person, not a professor performing expertise. When she explains a complex political situation, she doesn't start with the abstract principle. She starts with the story: Who did what? Why did it matter? What happened next? The pattern comes afterward, once you've already understood the human drama. This is the opposite of how most history is taught. Most textbooks start with the generalization and then give examples. Richardson starts with the example and lets the generalization emerge.

Here's her secret: She connects every historical lesson to something happening right now. Not in a forced way—in a way that makes you realize the connection was always there. When students ask "Why does this matter?", Richardson's approach has a built-in answer. It matters because it's still happening. The Reconstruction era isn't over. The debates about democracy from the 1850s are debates we're having today. History stops feeling like a list of dead people and becomes a tool for understanding the living.

For future teachers, Richardson models something essential: You can be an expert without being intimidating. You can know a lot and still be approachable. You can have strong opinions and still trust your students to form their own. As she puts it: "If you do your job really well, nobody needs you anymore. And I am a teacher at heart."
